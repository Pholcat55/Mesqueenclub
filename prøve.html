The Camp nou

Bygging av Camp Nou

Camp Nou took over from FC Barcelona's former stadium, Camp de Les Corts, in 1957. A proposal for a new stadium had already been made
in 1948, but it wasn't until Hungarian star Ladislao Kubala joined the club that people started talking about Barça had grown from the
Camp de Les Corts. Camp de Les Corts was completed in 1922, and after
several major developments, a total of 60,000 spectators were squeezed in, a number that was seen as unjustified to exceed. It was therefore
decided at the board meeting on 19 September 1950 that a new plot of land should be purchased for development. A plot of
land next to Maternitat was acquired, close to the old Les Corts stadium. However, it did not go as planned. Just over six months later, several
of the members of the commission responsible for planning the new stadium began to change their minds about the choice of location.
Instead of Maternitat, there was a desire for a property on Diagonal. This led to the project being put on hold until Francesc Miró-Sans won
the presidential election in 1953. The new stadium became his most
important cause, and in February 1954 it was decided to build on the original property at Maternitat and not at Diagonal. On 28 March 1954,
in front of 60,000 fans, Miró-Sans laid the first brick of the new Camp Nou stadium. The responsibility for the design of the new stadium
was placed in the hands of the architects Francesc Mitjans Miró (Miró-Sans' cousin), Josep
Soteras Mauri and Lorenzo García Barbón. Just over a year later, the contract for the construction itself was given to INGAR SA,
which was to take 66.6 million pesetas for a job of 18 months. As with many other large construction projects, the budget was also exceeded
here. When the construction was finished, a sum of approximately 288
million pesetas was calculated, which the club had to finance with loans of over 160 million pesetas. The stadium was financed, but
with the purchase you got a debt that you were forced to pay for several years afterwards.
